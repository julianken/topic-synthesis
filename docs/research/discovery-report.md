## 1. Problem statement, the opportunity / white space, and a unit-economics anchor

**The problem.** Building a high-quality, interactive, scaffolded curriculum (the "ai-concept-viz" artifact: 100+ standalone HTML/Canvas/SVG pages, one per concept, in a tiered hub) is artisanal, slow, and expert-dependent. The thesis of topic-synthesis is that a multi-agent workflow can *generate* that artifact automatically from any topic + settings.

**The white space is precise and real.** The prior-art research (high overall trust) maps the market into five clusters, and the intersection topic-synthesis targets is genuinely unoccupied:

1. **AI tutors/copilots** (Khanmigo, Coursera Coach, Duolingo Max, Sana, Synthesis) — conversational adapters bolted onto *pre-authored* human content. They orchestrate dialogue over a fixed corpus; none generates a fresh scaffolded curriculum per topic. **Implication: do not build a chatbot.** That space is crowded and well-funded, and the moat ("works for ANY topic with no pre-authored corpus") is exactly what every tutor lacks.
2. **Interactive-explainer platforms** (Mathigon, Nicky Case's Explorable Explanations, 3Blue1Brown) — embody *exactly* the target pedagogy and artifact format, but are 100% human-authored. **This is the design north star, not a competitor.** The honest pitch is "Explorable Explanations / Mathigon, auto-generated for any topic."
3. **Notebook/synthesis tools** (NotebookLM) — generate study guides, mind maps, flashcards, audio/video from *user-supplied* sources. Verification corrected one detail: NotebookLM is bounded to user-*curated* sources, which can include web pages the user adds, not strictly local uploads. Either way the output is summarization-shaped and passive, not interactive coded explainers.
4. **Generative course/roadmap builders** (roadmap.sh, Coursebox, Coursera Program Builder) — already turn a topic into structure, but emit text outlines/slides/quizzes for instructors, not Canvas/SVG explorables. **This is the most direct competitive set and it is racing to free.** Do not win on outline generation.
5. **The generation primitive** (Claude interactive visualizations / Artifacts; Manim-from-prompt) — proves single interactive explainers are within current model capability. **Honesty correction from verification:** the claim that this "went mainstream in 2026" is overstated — Claude Artifacts launched mid-2024 and the pattern was established through 2025; 2026 is when it *matured* (persistent storage, in-artifact API calls, MCP). The substantive half holds: it has only been demonstrated one-explainer-at-a-time, with no curriculum structure, scaffolding, or status tracking.

**The opportunity** is the *orchestration layer above single artifacts*: multi-agent ANALYSIS → a tiered, status-tracked SITEMAP → many coordinated, consistent, scaffolded explainer pages. Single-page generation is near-commodity; cross-page coherence, the hub, prerequisite ordering, and "built vs soon" status are the defensible product.

**The existential risk is baked into the same finding** (high confidence, supported in verification): AI-generated educational content confidently *fabricates concepts and renders flawed visuals* (the Manim demos "will happily explain non-existing concepts," stated TREE(3) = 27, drew a flawed Cantor function). Matching hand-built quality is argued to need expert-level verification AI cannot self-supply. This is why the founder's eval + A/B requirements are **load-bearing, not optional** — they are the only thing separating this product from the discredited demos.

**A concrete cost model — because §10 risk #4 (cost blow-up) is called existential and the founder cannot price without a dollar figure.** The numbers below are a *back-of-envelope*, not a measured benchmark — they exist to make the pricing/segment decision tractable and to be replaced by `count_tokens()` + real run telemetry before any pricing commitment. Pricing is from the claude-api skill (cached 2026-06-04): **Opus 4.8 $5 / $25 per 1M input/output tokens; Sonnet 4.6 $3 / $15; Haiku 4.5 $1 / $5; Batches API −50%; prompt-cache reads ~0.1× input.** Assume a mid-size curriculum of **40 built pages** (within the 100+ ceiling), tiered models per §4.

| Stage | Model | Calls | ~In tok/call | ~Out tok/call | ~Cost |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Planner + STORM question-asking | Opus 4.8 | 1 | 6k | 4k | $0.13 |
| Researcher fan-out (agentic RAG) | Sonnet 4.6 | 4 | 30k | 6k | $0.72 |
| Graph-builder | Opus 4.8 | 1 | 20k | 8k | $0.30 |
| Citation/grounding verification | Sonnet 4.6 | 1 | 25k | 4k | $0.14 |
| Per-node synth: spec (draft) | Sonnet 4.6 | 40 | 8k (cached prefix) | 3k | $2.76 |
| Per-node synth: page code | Sonnet 4.6 | 40 | 10k (cached prefix) | 8k | $6.00 |
| Critic loop (rubric, ~1.5 iters avg) | Opus 4.8 | 60 | 14k | 5k | $11.70 |
| Eval-judge passes (offline, gating) | Opus 4.8 | 40 | 12k | 2k | $4.40 |
| Hub assembler | Sonnet 4.6 | 1 | 15k | 4k | $0.11 |
| **Total (per 40-page curriculum)** | | | | | **~$26** |

That is roughly **$0.65/page all-in**, dominated by the **Critic loop (~45%)** and **eval-judge passes (~17%)** — the two stages a cost-cutting pass should target first. Three levers move it materially: (a) routing first-draft node synthesis and grounding to the **Message Batches API** (non-latency-sensitive) halves those line items (~−$4); (b) **prompt caching** the shared graph/brief prefix across the 40 nodes is already assumed and is the difference between $26 and ~$40+ — a silent cache miss here (see §6 model-drift, §9) roughly doubles cost; (c) reserving Opus for Planner/Graph/Critic only, as modeled. **The decision this forces:** at ~$26 of variable cost per full curriculum, the **$4/mo Khanmigo-style anchor and free roadmap.sh anchor are non-starters for per-curriculum-from-scratch generation** — one curriculum eats six months of a $4 subscription. The viable segments are **prosumer/B2B at a price that clears $26 + margin + a regeneration allowance**, or a **shared-page-library model** (§4, §10) that amortizes generation across many learners. This is the single most decision-shaping output of the report and should anchor the pricing conversation.

**One genuine differentiator on the demand side:** even Khanmigo lacks gold-standard RCT efficacy evidence (medium confidence; a 2025 study found *no* significant difference vs. plain Google search), so "we can show which workflow produces measurably better learning" is a credible, defensible claim — *if* the instrumentation is rigorous (§7), and a backfire risk if it shows no advantage.

## 2. The target artifact, the quality bar, accessibility, and what happens when retrieval is thin

**The artifact (Grounding A).** Each concept is a standalone triplet: `{slug}.html` (300–700 LOC) + `{slug}.js` (400–800 LOC) + optional `{slug}.css`. Zero npm dependencies; vanilla SVG/Canvas with imperative JS; loads and renders sub-1s. A single `window.SITEMAP` object (tier → title → categories → pages, each with `slug`, `title`, `built` boolean) is the source of truth that drives both the hub index and per-page sibling navigation.

**The four quality pillars** (the bar generation must clear):
- **Pedagogical scaffolding** — each page progresses intuition → worked example → reflection via numbered band sections.
- **Interactive-first visualization** — SVG/Canvas respond instantly to slider/button input, no reloads, `prefers-reduced-motion` respected.
- **Code-to-diagram binding** — live, syntax-highlighted code updates in lockstep with the diagram; real math happening, no undefined values or syntax errors.
- **Semantic typographic clarity** — consistent CSS variables encoding a metaphor (cyan = external/lookup, amber = weights/internal).

**A fifth pillar the artifact spec under-specifies and the buyer will demand: accessibility of the *generated* page.** `prefers-reduced-motion` (covered in §8) protects the *chrome*; it does nothing for the generated explorable. **Canvas content is invisible to assistive technology by default** — a `<canvas>` is an opaque bitmap to a screen reader, and SVG without `role`/`<title>`/`<desc>` is nearly as opaque. A model that emits a slick Canvas visualization with no text alternative produces a page that is unusable for blind learners and **fails WCAG 2.1 — a hard procurement gate and legal-exposure item for any institutional/education buyer (Section 508, EN 301 549, ADA-adjacent risk).** This is not a polish item; for a B2B/education segment it is a go/no-go. Concrete mitigations, all of which must be *generation constraints and eval rubric items*, not afterthoughts:
- Every `<canvas>` interaction must have a parallel accessible representation: an adjacent live-region text summary of the current state, a real `<table>`/`<dl>` of the underlying data, and keyboard-operable controls (sliders/buttons as native focusable elements, not div-with-onclick).
- Every `<svg>` carries `role="img"` + `<title>`/`<desc>`, or is built from focusable, labeled elements.
- All interactivity is keyboard-reachable and the tab order is sane; visible focus indicators survive the model's CSS.
- A deterministic **axe-core / Lighthouse-accessibility headless pass** runs in the same render-check harness as §5's visualization-fidelity check, and **a failing a11y score is a hard gate** (degrade the page to "soon", not "built"). Add an explicit **Accessibility** dimension to the synthesis rubric (§5).

**How generation maps — and where it does NOT.** The grounding's own implications are candid and should be taken at face value:
- Standalone HTML triplets with no cross-page dependency except auto-generated `sitemap.js` / `nav.js` map cleanly to a **map-reduce-per-node generator**.
- The sitemap-as-source-of-truth maps cleanly to a **curriculum-graph contract** (see §3–4).
- **But:** "Interactive visualizations require hand-coded SVG/Canvas logic tailored to each concept. Generic visualization templates will fail." And: "Pedagogical quality cannot be automated... the progression... the choice of which math to expose versus hide... are hand-crafted." The grounding explicitly states the generator "cannot produce a finished curriculum in one pass" — it produces a skeletal sitemap and bare-bones templates that experts then fill.

**The honest reconciliation of this tension with the prior-art finding.** The prior-art research says single-artifact generation is near-commodity (Claude Artifacts); the grounding says per-concept interactive logic is hand-crafted and un-templatable. Both are true at different altitudes: a *single* explorable page is feasible to generate, but generating one that actually teaches a *specific* concept correctly degrades exactly where the prior-art "domain coverage cliff" predicts (STEM/quantitative topics generate manipulable visuals; humanities may degrade to text). **The realistic v1 posture is generation + heavy verification + a "built vs soon" tier that gates cost and lets low-confidence pages stay as stubs** rather than pretending every page is finished.

**Cold-start / thin-coverage topics are a routine input, not an edge case — and the pipeline must have an explicit policy.** A niche topic ("the regulatory history of a small municipality's zoning code"; a brand-new framework with no docs) returns *thin* ANALYSIS retrieval. The product cannot silently fabricate to fill the gap — that is precisely the existential failure mode. The graph-builder and grounding-verification stages must compute a **coverage/confidence score per node** (e.g., number of independent, on-topic, verifiable sources backing each claim) and route on it:
1. **Sufficient coverage** → normal synthesis.
2. **Thin coverage** → **degrade gracefully to a text-and-citation page** (no fabricated interactive viz) marked with a low-confidence learner-facing badge (§7), *or* keep the node as **"soon"** in the sitemap rather than shipping a confident-looking wrong page.
3. **Near-zero coverage / topic largely absent from retrievable sources** → **refuse or narrow**: surface to the user at the §3 review gate ("we found limited reliable material on X; we can cover the adjacent, better-sourced Y, or proceed with a text-only, lightly-sourced outline") rather than generating a plausible-looking fabrication.
4. **Optionally route thin-coverage topics to a different, retrieval-light workflow** (e.g., a "from first principles, explicitly flagged as un-sourced reasoning" mode) — but only behind an explicit user choice and an explicit badge.

This makes "thin coverage" a first-class, surfaced state in the data model (a `coverage_confidence` field on every node and a job-level `low_confidence_node_count`), feeding both the A/B guardrails (§6) and the learner trust signals (§7).

## 3. Recommended product shape

**Intake (the settings).** A 3–4 step wizard (progressive disclosure, per-step Zod validation in a single wrapping `<form>`, partial state persisted to localStorage so a refresh doesn't lose input, expensive job gated behind a review step):
1. **Topic** (free text) — *also the first content-moderation surface; see the admissibility gate below.*
2. **Prior knowledge / depth** — *the single most consequential setting* because of the expertise-reversal effect (§5). A self-reported slider is coarse; strongly consider a short diagnostic pre-test (which doubles as retrieval practice and as a pre-period covariate for §6 CUPED).
3. **Desired format + time budget** — the Planner translates time budget into researcher count and critique-iteration caps (§4).
4. **Review + launch** — confirm the proposed curriculum outline (and any low-confidence/thin-coverage flags from §2) before generating all pages. A natural human-in-the-loop gate; maps to the AI SDK `needsApproval` flag, and the place where a thin-coverage topic gets surfaced rather than silently fabricated.

**Topic admissibility — a from-any-topic public generator needs a moderation gate, and quality evals do not cover this.** "Any topic" invites harmful, dangerous, or age-inappropriate inputs (self-harm methods, weapons/explosives synthesis, extremist recruitment material, authoritative-sounding medical/legal/financial misinformation presented as a confident tutorial). The §5 quality evals target *fabrication and pedagogy* — they will happily pass a well-scaffolded, beautifully-rendered, *dangerous* curriculum. A separate **admissibility classifier runs on the topic + settings before any expensive generation**, with three outcomes: allow, refuse-with-explanation, or allow-with-constraints (e.g., high-sensitivity domains like medical/legal get a mandatory "not professional advice; consult a licensed professional" frame and stricter grounding thresholds). Two implementation notes grounded in the claude-api material: (a) the model's own `stop_reason: "refusal"` with `stop_details.category` is a real signal to log and surface, but it is *not* a substitute for an explicit topic gate — it fires unevenly and can false-positive on benign adjacent work (security education, life-sciences); (b) build the gate as a discrete, versioned, evaluable component (its own small labeled set) so it can be tuned and audited like any other classifier. Age-appropriateness should be a settings dimension, not an afterthought.

**What gets generated.** A tiered, status-tracked **curriculum hub** (the ai-concept-viz format): tiers → categories → bite-size interactive pages, each with `built`/`soon` status. Each built page carries: a Bloom-verb learning objective, an interactive visualization that does real cognitive work, an embedded comprehension check, cited grounding for every factual claim, an accessible alternative (§2), and learner-facing trust signals (§7).

**The curriculum-graph model (the core data object).** The ANALYSIS phase outputs an explicit **prerequisite knowledge graph**: typed nodes (Concept / Skill / Exercise / Section) carrying a target Bloom level, with `prerequisite_for` edges. This is **well-grounded in learning science** (ZPD + knowledge-graph sequencing) as the *pedagogical model*. Its **LLM feasibility is medium confidence** (per the agent-architecture research; LLMs can infer prerequisite edges, best via a *hybrid* expert-seed + inference strategy) — that is a real caveat I carry forward into §4 rather than letting the strength of the pedagogical grounding bleed into a claim about the generator's reliability. The graph is the durable intermediate that:
- topologically sorts into the tier/category sitemap (synthesis can never place a concept before its prerequisites);
- supplies per-node briefs to the synthesizers;
- doubles as a learner-facing concept map (concept maps are an effective constructive activity — verification *strengthened* this, g ≈ 0.6–0.74 for constructing maps);
- is the unit for per-node engagement data and evals, and carries the `coverage_confidence` field from §2.

**Validate the graph as a hard gate before any synthesis runs:** topological sort succeeds (no cycles), every node reachable, depth/breadth matches settings, every node has verified citations or is flagged thin-coverage. A wrong graph cascades a difficulty cliff into every downstream page and is hard to detect from page-local evals — a named high-leverage failure point.

## 4. The multi-agent ANALYSIS → SYNTHESIS pipeline

The agent-architecture research (high overall trust) converges on a **two-phase plan-and-execute pipeline with the curriculum graph as the contract between phases.**

**A credit-where-it's-due correction.** Anthropic's multi-agent research system validates the **orchestrator-worker pattern for the research/breadth-first-search task specifically** (their reported +90.2% over single-agent at ~15× chat tokens is an internal eval, not peer-reviewed). It does **not** validate "a prerequisite-DAG-as-the-inter-phase contract" — that graph-as-contract design is *our* architectural inference, and its LLM-feasibility is medium-confidence (§3). So I apply Anthropic's evidence only to the ANALYSIS fan-out, and treat the graph contract as a design bet to be de-risked by the §3 hard-gate validation and §5 structure rubric, not as something with borrowed empirical backing.

**ANALYSIS sub-pipeline** (orchestrator-worker, the pattern Anthropic's system supports *for research*; reserve fan-out for the breadth-first stage only, because of the ~15× cost — both the +90.2% and 15× figures verified exactly, with the caveat that 90.2% is one company's internal eval):
1. **Planner** — decompose the topic; STORM-style perspective-guided question-asking to broaden coverage before researching. (STORM is the closest published analogue and is verified-supported.)
2. **Parallel Researcher subagents** (3–5, scaled to topic breadth) — **agentic RAG** (search → evaluate quality → refine query → re-retrieve), isolated context windows, returning 1–2k-token *condensed* summaries, not raw transcripts. **Each summary records source provenance + a coverage signal** to feed §2's thin-coverage routing and §7's citations.
3. **Graph-builder** — assemble the prerequisite DAG with typed nodes + per-node briefs + `coverage_confidence`.
4. **Citation/grounding verification pass** — a dedicated step that ties every claim to a source *before* it enters the graph. **This is where grounding lives, and it must be a hard gate.** It also computes per-node coverage for thin-topic routing.

**SYNTHESIS sub-pipeline** (map-reduce over graph nodes via LangGraph `Send()`-style dynamic fan-out):
5. **Per-node Synthesizer** — spec-then-code (ViviDoc DocSpec: State/Render/Transition/Constraint reviewable before code). Honesty note: ViviDoc is a single recent demo paper; the *architecture* exists but "is best done this way" is the researcher's design inference, not a benchmarked result. Still the most defensible pattern: the spec is cheaper to critique and gives a clean A/B surface. The spec must include an **accessibility contract** (§2) so the page-code step has it as a target, not a retrofit.
6. **Critic** (Reflexion-style reflection gated by an analytic rubric, **capped iterations** to bound cost — this is the single largest cost line, ~45% per §1). **This is where critique lives.** Persisting critique notes across nodes lets later pages avoid earlier mistakes. Open question to resolve in design: when a page fails, regenerate the *spec* (cheap) or the *page* (fixes render/run/a11y bugs the spec critique can't see)?
7. **Hub assembler** — reduce per-node outputs into the tiered sitemap with built/'soon' status.

**The unit-of-generation decision propagates through this whole section — and the report must say how.** §10 names "per-curriculum vs. reusable shared concept pages" as driving cost *and* A/B traffic density; the model in §1 and the pipeline above are written for **per-curriculum** generation. A **shared-page-library** model (generate each canonical concept page once, key it by `(concept, settings-bucket)`, and *compose* curricula by referencing existing pages) changes this pipeline concretely:
- The Synthesizer becomes a **cache-lookup-then-generate-on-miss** step keyed by a normalized concept identity + settings bucket; the per-curriculum variable cost collapses toward the *number of cache misses*, not 40 fresh pages. Over a library's lifetime this can take the marginal curriculum from ~$26 toward single dollars.
- The **Postgres schema** changes (see §9): `pages` is no longer FK-owned by one `job`; it becomes a shared `concept_pages` table with a many-to-many `curriculum_pages` join, plus a content-identity key for dedup.
- The **A/B randomization unit and traffic density** change (see §6): a shared page is seen by many learners across many curricula, so a page-level or workflow-version-level experiment accumulates power far faster than a per-curriculum design ever could — which is the single biggest lever on the §6 power problem.
- **Caching/invalidation** gets a new failure mode: a shared page generated under model snapshot *vN* is silently inconsistent with pages generated under *vN+1* (see §6/§9 model-drift).

I do **not** recommend hard-coding per-curriculum into the schema in v1. Build the data model so a page can be *referenced* by more than one curriculum from day one (cheap to do early, expensive to retrofit), even if v1 generates fresh every time — this preserves the shared-library option, which §1's cost model and §6's power problem both argue is likely the right long-run unit.

**Cost discipline (high priority, with the math from §1 behind it).** Tiered model strategy: strong model (`claude-opus-4-8`) for Planner, Graph-builder, Critic; cheaper models (Sonnet/Haiku) for Researcher execution and first-draft node synthesis. Bound synthesis fan-out concurrency to respect rate limits; route non-latency-sensitive bulk page synthesis and grounding through the **Message Batches API (−50%)**. Cache the shared graph/brief prefix across nodes (a silent cache miss roughly doubles cost — wire a `cache_read_input_tokens > 0` assertion into monitoring per §6/§9).

**IP / copyright / source-licensing — the "cited grounding" promise has a legal underside.** ANALYSIS scrapes and grounds on web sources. Two obligations the report previously ignored: (a) **reproduction** — a generated page that quotes or closely paraphrases substantial copyrighted source text (or embeds a scraped figure) is a reproduction, not a citation, and creates copyright exposure; the synthesis prompts and the grounding rubric (§5) must push toward *synthesis in original wording with attribution* and explicitly forbid verbatim block-copying of sources, with a deterministic near-duplicate check against retrieved text as a guard. (b) **attribution/licensing** — "cited grounding" implies we *show* sources; we should record each source's URL and, where detectable, its license/robots posture, and surface attributions on the page (which doubles as a §7 trust signal). This is also a buyer-trust and a brand-risk item, not only a legal one. Treat scraped content as *evidence to be re-expressed and attributed*, never as *content to be reproduced*.

**Make every intermediate artifact persistent and addressable** (research bundle with provenance, graph, per-node spec, generated page, critique notes). This buys resumable execution, single-node regeneration, incremental soon→built status, and is the substrate for per-node telemetry and workflow A/B. Anthropic explicitly flags that long multi-agent runs are hard to restart and errors compound — persistence is the mitigation.

## 5. Eval strategy for ANALYSIS & SYNTHESIS quality

The eval research (high overall trust) gives a repeatable recipe and one hard correction: the claim that reference-free LLM-as-judge is "the ONLY practical way" to score with no ground truth was rated **mixed** — NLI-based (SummaC/FactCC) and QA-based (QuestEval/QAGS) reference-free metrics are established alternatives, often cheaper as guardrails. LLM-as-judge is the most *flexible* option, not the only one.

**Split evals by stage — they are different problems.**
- **ANALYSIS quality is the tractable, label-free part:** treat it as groundedness/faithfulness against retrieved sources (Ragas-style faithfulness, MiniCheck/AlignScore-class NLI checkers). Score every claim-source pair; hard-fail synthesis if unsupported claims exist.
- **SYNTHESIS quality is the near-open problem:** rubric-based LLM-as-judge + deterministic guards + (eventually) real learner outcomes.

### Starter rubric (named dimensions, binary pass/fail + written critique — *not* 1–5 Likert)

The practitioner consensus (Hamel Husain) and the learning-science research both favor binary criteria with critiques (more actionable, less gameable). Aggregate to a per-artifact pass-rate.

**Analysis-quality rubric:**
1. **Groundedness** — every claim supported by a cited source.
2. **Coverage** — all core subtopics/prerequisites identified; nothing critical missing.
3. **Correctness** — no factual errors vs. world knowledge.
4. **Structure** — decomposition is a valid prerequisite DAG (topo-sort succeeds).
5. **Settings-fit** — depth/scope matches prior-knowledge & time-budget.
6. **Source legitimacy & non-reproduction** — sources are real and verifiable; no substantial verbatim copying of copyrighted text (§4 IP).

**Synthesis-quality rubric:**
1. **Accuracy** — faithful to the analysis; no new hallucinations.
2. **Coverage** — each tier/category page covers its concept.
3. **Scaffolding** — prerequisites before dependents; difficulty increases; guidance matches the declared learner level (expertise-reversal: a one-size page should be *penalized*).
4. **Clarity** — plain, well-structured.
5. **Readability-band** (deterministic) — Flesch-Kincaid lands in the band implied by the prior-knowledge setting. **Guardrail only, never a quality score** — FK ignores vocabulary/coherence and disagrees across formulas.
6. **Engagement / active recall** — includes a retrieval/check-for-understanding step. The ICAP-grounded target is to climb to at least **Constructive** (predict-then-reveal, self-explanation *prompt* rather than provide; g ≈ 0.55, verified-supported). Note: AI is documented as weakest on metacognition, so test for it explicitly. (Honesty: "weakest on metacognition" is a recurring concern, not a settled empirical ranking.)
7. **Visualization-fidelity** — the viz actually illustrates the concept and **renders/runs without errors** (deterministic headless-browser render check + an LLM/multimodal judgment that it does cognitive work, not decoration). The single check that most separates "looks built" from "is built."
8. **Accessibility (deterministic + judged)** — axe-core/Lighthouse-a11y headless pass clears threshold; Canvas/SVG have text alternatives; controls are keyboard-operable (§2). **A failing a11y score is a hard gate → page stays "soon".**

**On the embedded comprehension check as both intervention and metric — an honesty correction.** The retrieval-practice mechanism is well-established in the learning-science literature *in general*, but the specific "roughly a letter grade higher" figure in the finding rests on a **single classroom result**, and elsewhere this report is careful to hedge effect sizes. I apply the same discipline here: use the embedded check as a *leading behavioral signal* (a page whose own check learners systematically fail is, by evidence, poorly synthesized — §7), but **do not treat any single immediate-check pass rate as proof of learning**, and do not let one classroom effect size set product expectations. The verdict metric is delayed retention/transfer (§7), not the in-page check.

### Handling the no-ground-truth problem
1. **Build the gold set FIRST**, before optimizing the generator — ~50–100 diverse (topic × settings) inputs, labeled by one pedagogy-expert "benevolent dictator" + 1–2 others, with written critiques. (See §9 for how this reconciles with "ship a curriculum first.")
2. **Calibrate the judge as a first-class versioned artifact:** align to human labels until Cohen's kappa clears ~0.6 (target >0.8), measure with **TPR/TNR not accuracy** (imbalanced labels), recalibrate monthly, refresh the gold set quarterly. Kappa <0.4 means the *rubric* is ambiguous — rewrite it. Maintain a 100–200 item human-labeled calibration set to estimate the judge's TPR/FPR and apply variance-corrected thresholds ("Noisy but Valid").
3. **De-bias every judge call:** run pairwise in both orders (or score-then-rank), instruct the judge to penalize verbosity, and **use a judge from a different model family than the generator** to blunt self-preference. Cross-family is the real fix; a judge pool sharing model lineage shares self-preference.
4. **Rank workflows pairwise, not on absolute averages:** Bradley-Terry/Elo aggregation with active sampling on close calls (Chatbot Arena pattern; verified-supported, with the noted O(n²) cost and homogeneous-evaluator assumption).
5. **Offline rubric = leading indicator; learner outcomes = verdict.** No offline metric is ground truth for "did this person learn." Eval and output-telemetry (§7) are designed together.

**The SME is an operational dependency, not a free input — name it as a risk.** This whole program depends on a recurring human pedagogy expert: the "benevolent dictator" who anchors the gold set, *monthly* judge recalibration, *quarterly* gold-set refresh, and an ongoing SME annotation queue (§7) to convert production failures into regression cases. For a solo founder that is a **single point of failure and a recurring cost** — if the SME is unavailable for a month, judge drift goes uncorrected and every live experiment's verdict quietly degrades. Mitigations: (a) front-load the heaviest labeling (initial gold set) when SME attention is cheapest; (b) make recalibration *cadenced and small* (100–200 items) so it fits a few hours/month; (c) design the annotation queue to prioritize the highest-signal cases (low-engagement/high-failure pages, §7) so SME time is spent where it moves the metric; (d) treat "SME hours available this month" as an explicit input to whether new experiments can be trusted. Budget this as a line item, not as goodwill.

**Tooling:** Braintrust as platform of record (a `bt` CLI skill is available in this environment) + promptfoo/DeepEval in CI to gate PRs + Ragas-style faithfulness for the analysis stage. Don't build eval infra from scratch.

## 6. Robust A/B testing of whole workflows — with an honest power reality check

The A/B research (high overall trust) makes the central move clear: **treat the entire pipeline as a single, versioned, immutable artifact and make that version the experiment arm.**

**Randomization unit.** For per-curriculum generation, the unit is the **learner/session** (not the request), because one learner consumes one whole generated curriculum end-to-end; sticky assignment so each learner sees exactly one workflow version. **Under a shared-page-library model (§4) the unit can instead be the page or the page-version**, which is the single biggest improvement to the power problem below — a shared page is seen across many curricula and many learners. Whichever unit, assign server-side via deterministic hashing (GrowthBook/Statsig pattern — verified-supported), recorded on every job so every downstream telemetry/eval record is attributable.

**A workflow version = an arm.** Define `WorkflowVersion = content-hash(DAG + every prompt + model IDs + params + tool/retrieval config + transitions-dev render templates)`. The A/B flag returns that hash; the orchestrator pins every agent call to it; randomization is by the chosen unit.

**Critical non-immutability trap — and a monitoring requirement the report previously left implicit.** Foundation models update underneath you, and prompt caching is per-model: changing model/tools/system-prompt invalidates the cache *and* breaks reproducibility. So **model + tools + prompts live inside the version unit and are pinned to dated snapshots**. But pinning is not enough: **dated/pinned model snapshots get deprecated and retired** — a near-certain event (the claude-api migration material lists real retirement dates). When a pinned snapshot is retired out from under a live experiment, the arm silently changes or starts erroring, **invalidating in-flight results and frozen workflow versions.** This needs active monitoring, not just a pin: (a) a scheduled job that queries the Models API for the deprecation/retirement status of every snapshot referenced by any *active* `workflow_version`, alerting before the retirement date; (b) a `cache_read_input_tokens > 0` assertion on the hot path (a silent cache miss is the early symptom of a snapshot/prompt drift and the §1 cost-doubler); (c) on a forced model change, **invalidate affected experiments and shared pages explicitly** rather than letting them bleed (§9 cache invalidation). Treat "the pinned model still exists and still caches" as a guardrail metric, not an assumption.

**Variance reduction — CUPED, framed honestly for *this* product.** CUPED is built into Statsig/Eppo by default (GrowthBook configurable). Microsoft reported ~50% variance reduction in *their* setting — but **CUPED's benefit is entirely contingent on a pre-period covariate that correlates with the outcome, and for a product whose learners are mostly first-time/anonymous that covariate is often weak or absent.** So I am **not** going to headline CUPED as "the highest-leverage technique" — for the anonymous-first-time case its realistic gain is modest. It earns its place only if we *manufacture* a usable covariate: the diagnostic pre-test from §3 (baseline quiz score) and any pre-experiment engagement (prior completion rate for returning learners). Capture that covariate deliberately; if it doesn't correlate with the outcome on real data, CUPED contributes little and we should not pretend otherwise. The genuinely highest-leverage variance lever for this product is **increasing the unit count** — i.e., the shared-page-library model and offline evals/interleaving carrying weight (below) — not a variance-reduction formula.

**The statistical-power reality check the report owes the founder.** Each observation is an expensive multi-agent generation, and long-tail topics may **never accumulate enough learners per variant for a valid online conclusion.** A back-of-envelope: detecting a ~5-percentage-point lift on a binary outcome (e.g., comprehension-check pass rate) at 80% power / α=0.05 needs *very roughly* ~1,500 learners *per arm* for that one comparison — and that is for the *aggregate*, not for a single niche topic that might see a handful of learners total. **Per-topic online A/B on learning outcomes is, for most of the long tail, infeasible.** The honest program is therefore tiered, and online A/B is the *narrow top* of it, not the workhorse:
1. **Offline evals (§5) are the primary decision surface** — they need no traffic and gate every workflow-version change.
2. **Interleaving** for ranking sub-decisions (which page/visualization to surface) — 10–100× sensitivity (verified-supported, *scoped to ranking, not learning outcomes*) — extracts signal from far less traffic.
3. **Topic bucketing + surrogate metrics** — pool sparse long-tail topics into buckets (by domain, by STEM/humanities, by coverage-confidence) and read *guardrails* and *engagement surrogates* at the bucket level, accepting that per-topic learning-outcome reads are out of reach.
4. **Online learner-outcome A/B is reserved for high-traffic head topics and aggregate workflow-level questions** ("does workflow B beat A across all topics") — and even there, **pre-register a fixed primary-readout horizon and a minimum-sample stopping rule, and accept that many experiments will end *underpowered/inconclusive* rather than significant.** A shared-page-library unit (§4) is what makes more of these reads reachable.

**Read results under a sequential framework — for early-stop *safety*, not for harvesting wins.** Peeking inflates false positives (5%/day compounds toward ~30% over a week). Statsig's mSPRT trades ~16% power for safe early stopping (verified-supported). Use sequential/always-valid CIs to **auto-stop clearly harmful arms on guardrails**; do not use them to declare a winner on the first green dashboard, and discount early-stopped positive effect sizes (they over-estimate).

**Guardrails on every experiment:** cost-per-curriculum (summed tokens × price across all agents — the §1 model is the budget), end-to-end generation latency, failure/refusal/empty-render/empty-a11y rate, and `low_confidence_node_count` (§2) — wired to the sequential framework so a clearly harmful or far-more-expensive arm auto-stops. A 4-agent quality bump that doubles cost (§1) is blocked here.

**Pre-screen cheaply before spending real traffic:** **AgentA/B-style synthetic LLM traffic** only as a pre-flight smoke test to kill obviously-worse variants; **require a real human-learner experiment for any learning-outcome ship decision** (where traffic exists per the tiering above).

**Honest methodological limit:** there is no peer-reviewed canonical standard for whole-agentic-workflow A/B testing. The program *composes* well-established pieces (unit randomization + a *correctly-scoped* CUPED + sequential analysis + guardrails + judge calibration + an offline-first power posture); design it as an assembly, not a turnkey method.

## 7. Output-side telemetry, delayed-outcome instrumentation, and learner-facing trust

The telemetry research (high overall trust) shows this is the keystone: **A/B needs an outcome metric and evals need ground-truth labels — output-side data supplies both.**

**One internal event schema, projectable to standards.** Borrow Caliper/xAPI vocabulary (verification confirmed Caliper 1.2 has exactly 15 profiles; soften "covers nearly every event" — bespoke canvas/SVG interactions fall back to General/ToolUse). A small *fixed-string* event set — `reading:section_view`, `media:viz_interact`, `assessment:item_answer`, `feedback:rating_submit`, `session:page_dwell`, `page:completed` — with `topic`, `page_slug`, `workflow_variant_id`, `generation_run_id`, `learner_pseudo_id`, `consent_status` in **properties, never in event names**. Validate at ingestion with JSON Schema.

**Instrumentation recipe** baked into the generation template so every page emits comparable telemetry: one `IntersectionObserver` per section and per visualization (section-view + viz-dwell + scroll-depth), a Page-Visibility-gated active-time timer (not wall-clock), and a single `navigator.sendBeacon` flush on `visibilitychange` (or terminal events — completion, last quiz answer — are lost on tab close, biasing metrics downward).

**The primary *online* outcome metric** should be a cheap, low-noise behavioral signal — completion, in-page quiz pass-rate, return/retention — with expensive LLM-judge scores relegated to secondary/offline. Per-item error distributions are as informative as pass rates.

**The hard cross-cutting caution from learning science** (high confidence, verified): **measure DELAYED retention/transfer, not just immediate engagement.** Desirable-difficulties research shows in-the-moment ease can be *negatively* correlated with durable learning (interleaving: 63% delayed vs. 20% blocked). An engagement-driven optimizer will select for fluent, low-difficulty, judge-pleasing material that *harms* learning (Goodhart). **Delayed retention is the true verdict metric — but the report previously said "build return-quiz instrumentation in" without saying HOW, and the HOW determines whether the entire A/B-on-learning-outcomes program is even feasible.** The honest answer: getting anonymous learners to come back for a delayed test at scale is *hard*, and you cannot rely on it for most of the long tail (this is the same scarcity that drives §6's power problem). Concrete mechanics, in descending order of how much they actually move return rates:
- **Spaced-review as a product feature, not a survey** — fold the delayed test *into* the learning experience: a "review session" surfaced N days later that the learner is intrinsically motivated to take because spaced review demonstrably helps them (and is itself good pedagogy). This converts the measurement burden into a value-add, which is the only mechanism likely to get meaningful voluntary return at scale.
- **Account/identity, even lightweight** — a returning-learner pseudo-identity (cookie or optional account) is the precondition for *any* longitudinal link; design the pseudo-ID and `consent_status` so a return visit can be joined to the original generation run. Anonymous-only sessions can supply immediate metrics but essentially cannot supply delayed ones.
- **Scheduled re-engagement** — email/push spaced-review reminders *for learners who opted in*, with the reminder framed as a study aid. Opt-in rate caps the program; size expectations accordingly.
- **Surrogate/leading indicators when return is unavailable** — within-session indicators of desirable difficulty (e.g., performance on *interleaved* vs. blocked items, success on transfer-style items inside the session) as imperfect proxies for the long-tail topics where no delayed measurement will ever arrive.
- **Realistic conclusion:** anchor A/B win criteria on lagging learning signals *where the cohort and traffic permit it* (head topics, opted-in returning learners), and on offline evals + within-session surrogates everywhere else. Do not design the program assuming delayed-retention data will materialize for arbitrary anonymous topics — it won't.

**The learner is an active stakeholder, not just a randomization unit and telemetry emitter — and that is also a quality defense.** Surface learner-facing trust signals on every page: **citations/attributions visible** (the §4 "cited grounding" promise made tangible, doubling as IP attribution), a **confidence/verification badge** reflecting the node's `coverage_confidence` and grounding-verification status (low-coverage pages from §2 are *labeled as such*, not disguised), and a **"this page is wrong / confusing / report a problem" button** on every page. That button is simultaneously (a) a credibility mechanism against the fabrication risk the report calls existential — learners can flag what slipped past the evals — and (b) a high-signal feedback channel that feeds the §5 annotation queue directly (a learner-flagged page is a near-certain eval case). Treating learners as collaborators in quality, not just data sources, is cheap to build and is a real mitigation for risk #1.

**The loop:** stamp shared trace IDs across generation and learner events → low-engagement/high-failure/learner-flagged pages auto-surface as candidate eval cases → SMEs annotate a *prioritized* sample (annotation queue, sized to fit the SME's monthly hours per §5) to build the labeled gold set → those become regression tests gating future workflow versions. Braintrust/LangSmith support this production-to-eval conversion today (verified-supported).

**Privacy is a hard constraint, framed honestly.** Verification *corrected* the strong legal claim: GDPR has no categorical "intervention requires explicit consent" rule. The real hard triggers are automated decisions with significant effect (Art. 22), special-category data (Art. 9), and ePrivacy storage-access. So: pseudonymous-by-default, `consent_status` as a first-class property, aggregate engagement under legitimate interest — a sound *conservative design posture*, not a statutory mandate. The return-quiz/account mechanics above raise the stakes here (a returning-learner identity is more linkable), so consent for re-engagement email/push must be explicit and the right-to-erasure path must propagate into the production events that have become permanent eval regression cases.

They do **not** discharge the accessibility obligation of the **generated Canvas/SVG pages** — that is a separate, larger problem handled as a generation constraint and a hard eval gate in §2/§5. Do not let "the chrome respects reduced-motion" be mistaken for "the artifacts are accessible."

**Chrome stack:** Next.js + React 19 + Tailwind v4 + shadcn/ui (shadcn handles structure/accessibility; transitions-dev handles motion — they compose via `:root` variables). Consider React Flow for the tiers→categories→pages sitemap, or a simpler responsive CSS grid.

**The single most load-bearing UI decision is NOT the chrome — it is how generated interactive artifacts are rendered safely.** The verified pattern (Claude Artifacts, reverse-engineered): a **sandboxed iframe served from a SEPARATE, cookieless origin** (`*.sandbox.<product>.com`), `postMessage` for control, DOMPurify on injected HTML, a single allowlisted CDN. Critical rules from verification:
- Use `sandbox="allow-scripts"` **without** `allow-same-origin`. Verification refined the mechanism: the self-removal escape applies to *same-origin* frames; cross-origin, `allow-same-origin` is dangerous because it hands the frame a usable real origin with storage/network access. Either way: omit it for untrusted generated code.
- **Enforce CSP as an HTTP response header on the sandbox origin** (server-controlled), not a `<meta>` tag the model emits. Verification *corrected* one nuance: a `<meta>` CSP actually *can't* be stripped by in-frame scripts (parse-time enforcement), so it's a meaningful barrier — but the stronger reasons to prefer server headers are coverage of all response types and avoidance of injection-before-meta ordering. The robust boundary is the **separate cookieless origin**, with CSP as defense-in-depth.
- Add automated escape tests (`javascript:` links, `window.opener`, parent-cookie read).

**Long-job UX:** SSE with typed progress events (stage-start, stage-complete, artifact-ready) — simpler and Vercel-compatible vs. WebSockets, which are only needed if learners must *steer* a run mid-flight. **Resumable streams so a learner can close the tab and return — but this is a medium-confidence, lock-in-bearing recommendation, not a load-bearing v1 must.** Honesty correction carried forward from verification: the resumable/durable primitives are delivered by **Vercel's Workflow DevKit, not AI SDK 6 *core*** (where `Agent` is just an interface). The DevKit's own maturity is **unverified**, and adopting it couples the product to Vercel. So I rank this as a *nice-to-have* whose dependency and lock-in must be weighed explicitly: the durable-workflow spine (§9, Trigger.dev/Inngest) can already give you resumable *jobs*; tab-close-and-return on the *stream* is a UX refinement on top, not the thing v1 lives or dies on. Do not present it as a settled core capability.

## 9. Technical architecture, orchestration, and an honest sequencing reconciliation

Bootstrapped from **agentic-seed** (Grounding B): create via "Use this template" on `julianken/agentic-seed`; fill 9 placeholders (PROJECT_NAME='Topic Synthesis', OWNER='julianken', REPO='topic-synthesis', etc.); adopt Mergify + review-bot + Figma modules. **DESIGN.md becomes the design source of truth** — populate the Token Manifest with transitions-dev tokens and the Motion section with the surface→transition mapping. The instance starts "in design — pre-code; no package.json/build/CI yet"; the anti-invention guardrail means agents must not claim build/test commands until the stack lands.

**The runtime architecture** (tech-architecture research, high overall trust):
- **Durable workflow engine as the spine** (Trigger.dev or Inngest — TypeScript-native, serverless-friendly, **step memoization so an expensive LLM call runs exactly once across crashes/retries**; verified-supported, with the precision that exactly-once applies to committed step results). Reserve Temporal for scale-up. Model the pipeline as discrete memoized steps: analyze → graph → sitemap → parallel fan-out per page → assemble hub.
- **Claude API directly with `claude-opus-4-8`** (Opus for Planner/Graph/Critic, Sonnet/Haiku for researchers/first-draft synthesis per §1/§4), orchestrated as **code-controlled workflow steps** (not open-ended Managed Agents) so the entire DAG is diffable, versionable, replayable — the precondition for §6 A/B and §5 evals. Honesty correction: "workflow tier vs Managed Agents" is an *architectural* distinction in Anthropic's guidance (code-orchestrated predefined paths vs. model-directed autonomy), not named billing tiers. Use structured outputs for the analysis sitemap.
- **SSE** sourced from the durable engine's realtime layer.
- **Postgres** (JSONB for evolving configs/eval-results/telemetry) as system-of-record. **The schema must be unit-of-generation-aware from day one (§4):** `workflow_versions` (immutable, content-addressed, *with a `model_snapshots` array so the §6 retirement-monitor can find them*); `jobs` (FK to version, A/B variant, status); **`concept_pages`** (content-identity key, tier/category, storage_key, built/soon, `coverage_confidence`) referenced by a **`curriculum_pages`** join table — so a page *can* be shared across curricula even if v1 generates fresh; `eval_runs`/`eval_scores`; `telemetry_events` (append-only). **Object storage (R2/S3)** for the immutable standalone HTML pages, keyed by content hash (not job+page), which is what makes the shared-library model a config change later rather than a rewrite.
- **Cache/version invalidation is a named operational job, not an assumption:** the §6 model-drift monitor, on a forced snapshot change, must mark affected `workflow_versions` stale and (under a shared library) flag pages generated under the retired snapshot for regeneration. Wire the `cache_read_input_tokens > 0` assertion into the engine's per-step logging.

**Sequencing for a solo/small team — reconciling the one real tension in the report.** Two earlier recommendations appear to conflict: §9 says "workflow engine + Postgres + SSE + sandbox first; evals + A/B second," while §5 says "build the gold set FIRST, before optimizing the generator." Both are right because they govern **different things on different clocks**, and a solo builder can honor both with this ordering:
1. **Generator skeleton first (weeks).** Stand up the durable-workflow spine, Postgres (with the share-ready schema), SSE, and the sandboxed-iframe renderer, and ship a *single end-to-end curriculum* you can look at. You cannot write a meaningful rubric or gold set without concrete artifacts to react to — "build the gold set first" does **not** mean "before any generation exists," it means "before *optimizing/iterating* the generator." A rubric written in the abstract is the §5 kappa<0.4 failure waiting to happen.
2. **Gold set + rubric second, *in parallel with the first iteration loop* (weeks).** The moment you have ~a handful of real curricula, the SME labels the first gold set and you freeze the rubric. From here on, **the gold set gates every generator change** — this is what "build it first" protects against: tuning the generator on vibes and Goodharting your way to worse output. Crucially, the offline gold set is also what makes §6 viable under low traffic (it's the primary decision surface), so it is not a luxury you defer until you have users.
3. **A/B + online telemetry third (once there is traffic).** Learner-outcome experimentation is gated on having learners; until then, offline evals + interleaving carry the weight (§6). The output-side telemetry schema (§7) can be wired early and cheaply (it's just events), but the *experimentation program* lights up only when traffic exists.

So the resolution is: **a thin generator before evals, the gold set before generator *optimization*, and online A/B last.** "Ship a curriculum first" and "build the gold set before the generator is good" are sequential, not contradictory — the gold set sits between "it runs" and "it's good," and is the gate for crossing that gap. The biggest sequencing risk for a solo builder is **not** picking a slightly-wrong vendor; it is **adopting durable engine + eval platform + experimentation platform + object storage all at once and stalling before any curriculum ships** — so the engine/Postgres/SSE/sandbox come first precisely so a real artifact exists for the gold set to be built *against*.

## 10. Key risks & open design questions

The hardest problems, ranked: **(1) quality/credibility collapse** from fabricated concepts and flawed visuals — the existential risk that makes evals load-bearing and that learner trust signals (§7) partially defend; **(2) the garbage-in curriculum graph** — a wrong prerequisite DAG silently cascades difficulty cliffs into every page and is invisible to page-local evals; **(3) optimizing the proxy instead of the learner** — engagement/judge-pleasing material that harms durable learning, the Goodhart trap that delayed-retention instrumentation (§7) exists to counter but can only partially reach given the return-rate problem; **(4) cost & pricing viability** — ~$26 variable cost per 40-page curriculum (§1) against $4/mo and free anchors makes per-curriculum consumer pricing a non-starter; the segment/unit decision is existential; **(5) the unit-of-generation decision** (per-curriculum vs. shared-page-library) — it drives cost (§1), the schema (§9), the A/B unit and *whether A/B has the power to conclude anything* (§6); **(6) statistical-power infeasibility on the long tail** — most topics will never see enough learners for valid online learning-outcome reads, forcing an offline-first program; **(7) accessibility of generated artifacts** — Canvas/SVG are invisible to assistive tech by default; a WCAG-failing generated page is a procurement and legal blocker for institutional buyers (§2); **(8) content-moderation / subject-matter safety** — a from-any-topic public generator can produce harmful/dangerous/misinforming-but-well-scaffolded curricula that quality evals will happily pass (§3); **(9) the artifact safety surface** — rendering model-generated HTML/JS wrong exposes user sessions (§8); **(10) SME availability as a single point of failure** — the eval+A/B program depends on recurring expert labeling and monthly recalibration (§5); **(11) model/vendor drift** — pinned model snapshots get retired, silently invalidating live experiments and frozen workflow versions, and Vercel Workflow DevKit lock-in for resumable streams (§6/§8/§9); **(12) IP/source-licensing** — reproduction of copyrighted source material and attribution obligations behind the "cited grounding" promise (§4); **(13) domain coverage cliff** — the interactive advantage may hold only for STEM, and thin-coverage niche topics need explicit refuse/degrade/flag routing (§2).

The most decision-shaping items to put on the brainstorming agenda first: **the unit of generation (risk #5)** — because it cascades into cost, schema, and whether A/B is even powered; **the buyer/segment and resulting price (risk #4)** — gated by the §1 cost model; **the visual-and-accessibility verification mechanism (risks #1, #7)** — the single thing separating "looks built" from "is built and usable"; the **topic-admissibility policy (risk #8)** for a public generator; and the **single north-star learning-outcome metric** given that delayed retention is the true verdict but is only reachable for part of the cohort (§7).
